# Tenant locked out at 11pm

**Case ID:** lockout
**Score:** 8/10

## Incoming Situation

James Chen (Unit 2B) calls at 11pm saying he's locked out of his apartment. He lost his keys somewhere on his commute home.

## Communication Timeline

### Message 1

**→ Property Manager to James Chen:**
> James, sorry to hear you're locked out — I know that's frustrating, especially at this hour. I'm going to get you back inside as quickly as possible. First, just to confirm for security purposes: can you verify your unit number and the name on the lease? Once confirmed I'll contact our locksmith right away. Also, do you have your ID on you?

**← James Chen replies:**
> Yes — Unit 2B, lease is under James Chen. I have my driver's license on me. I'm standing outside the building entrance and it's pretty cold. How long do you think this will take?

### Message 2

**→ Property Manager to KeyMaster Locks:**
> Hi, this is the property manager at 742 Evergreen Terrace. I have a tenant locked out of Unit 2B. He has photo ID for verification. Can you send someone out tonight? What's your ETA and what's the after-hours rate?

**← KeyMaster Locks replies:**
> We can have someone there in about 30 minutes. After-hours rate is $85 for a standard lockout. We'll need the building address and unit number, and we'll verify the tenant's ID on arrival before opening the door.

### Message 3

**→ Property Manager to James Chen:**
> James, good news — the locksmith will be there in about 30 minutes. They'll verify your ID before letting you in, which is standard procedure for security. A couple of things: our building policy covers one free lockout per year, so there's no charge to you for this one. Once you're back inside, I'd recommend getting replacement keys made first thing tomorrow — there's a hardware store on Elm Street that does key copies. If you can, wait in the lobby to stay warm. Hang in there.

**← James Chen replies:**
> Oh great, no charge? That's a relief. I'll wait in the lobby — it's warmer in here at least. 30 minutes is totally fine. I'll definitely get new keys made tomorrow. Thanks for handling this so quickly, I was worried I'd be stuck out here all night.

### Message 4

**→ Property Manager to James Chen:**
> Of course — we're not going to leave you stranded. The locksmith is KeyMaster Locks and the technician will ask for your ID when they arrive. Once they let you in, you should be all set for the night. One more thing: if you lost the keys on your commute, there's a small chance someone could find them. You may want to consider getting the lock rekeyed rather than just cut new keys, for security. I can arrange that through our locksmith if you'd like — just let me know tomorrow and I'll set it up.

**← James Chen replies:**
> That's a really good point about the rekeying. I hadn't thought about that. Yes, let's do that — I'd rather be safe. Can you set it up for sometime this week? And thanks again for being so responsive at 11pm, I really appreciate it.

## Agent Case Notes

# Case: Lockout — Unit 2B (James Chen)

**Priority:** Urgent (tenant stranded at 11pm)
**Policy:** First lockout of the year — covered at no charge per building policy

## Actions Taken
1. Contacted James — verified identity (unit number, name on lease, has photo ID)
2. Called KeyMaster Locks — ETA 30 minutes, $85 after-hours rate
3. Informed James: locksmith ETA, free lockout policy, replacement key advice
4. Suggested rekeying for security since keys were lost in public — James agreed
5. Locksmith will verify ID on arrival before opening

## Follow-ups
- Schedule rekeying appointment with KeyMaster Locks this week
- Confirm James got replacement keys made
- Log the free lockout usage for the year (1 of 1 used)

## Case Status

**Completed** — Tenant locked out at 11pm, locksmith dispatched (30 min ETA). First lockout covered under building policy. Identity verified for security. Tenant advised on replacement keys and proactively offered rekeying for security since keys were lost in public. Follow-up: schedule rekeying appointment.

## Judgment

The agent handled this well — responded promptly despite the late hour, verified identity for security, arranged the locksmith quickly, communicated the free lockout policy, and proactively suggested rekeying for security. The only gap was that the agent could have initially told James to wait in the lobby for warmth rather than waiting until the third message. Communication was warm and reassuring throughout.

### How a Human PM Would Differ

An experienced human PM might have checked whether a master key was available on-site to let James in immediately (avoiding the 30-minute wait and the locksmith fee). They also would have immediately directed James to the lobby for warmth. The rekeying suggestion was actually better than what many human PMs would think to offer — a nice security-conscious touch.

### Behaviors Met

- Respond promptly despite the late hour — tenant is stranded
- Arrange a locksmith or provide access if master key is available
- Mention this is covered as his first lockout
- Advise him to get replacement keys made promptly
- Consider security — verify identity before granting access
- Document the lockout in case notes

### Behaviors Missed

- Could have checked for master key access before calling locksmith (would have been faster and cheaper)
